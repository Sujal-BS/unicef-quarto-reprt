Vitamin A Supplementation: Saving Children's Lives Worldwide

Sujal Boodgere Sunil - A00096721 | BAA1030 UNICEF Data Analyst Report

1. Executive Summary

Vitamin A is an essential micronutrient required for healthy vision, strong immune function, and proper growth in children. Deficiency remains a significant public health challenge in many low- and middle-income countries, contributing to preventable blindness and increased mortality from common childhood infections.

Routine Vitamin A supplementation has been proven to reduce under-five mortality by up to 23%. This report examines global coverage trends, economic correlations, and links to child mortality using the latest UNICEF data, with the goal of raising awareness and supporting targeted interventions.

2. Why This Issue Matters

Despite global progress, Vitamin A deficiency continues to affect millions of children, particularly in regions with limited access to diverse diets. The consequences include irreversible blindness, weakened immunity, and higher vulnerability to diseases such as measles and diarrhoea. Effective supplementation programmes offer one of the most cost-effective ways to protect child health and support long-term development.

3. Key Findings

  • Global average coverage has shown steady improvement since 2000, yet substantial regional disparities persist.
  • A strong positive correlation exists between GDP per capita and Vitamin A supplementation coverage.
  • Several countries continue to report coverage rates below 10%, placing large numbers of children at serious risk.
  • A noticeable decline in coverage occurred in 2020, largely attributable to disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Countries with persistently low supplementation rates tend to experience higher child mortality in the 5–9 age group.

4. Data & Methodology

This analysis combines UNICEF Indicator 1 (Vitamin A Supplementation Coverage) and Indicator 2 (Mortality rate age 5–9) with World Bank metadata. Data processing—including cleaning, filtering, joining datasets, and summarisation—was performed using the Polars library in Python for efficiency and reproducibility. All visualisations were generated using the plotnine package.

6. Recommendations & Call to Action

To accelerate progress and protect more children, the following actions are recommended:

  • Strengthen supply chain logistics to ensure reliable and timely delivery of supplements, even in remote areas.
  • Integrate Vitamin A supplementation with routine immunisation and maternal-child health programmes.
  • Implement community-based awareness campaigns to improve parental understanding and uptake.
  • Provide targeted technical and financial support to countries with coverage rates below 50%.

Even small improvements in coverage can result in substantial reductions in preventable child mortality and blindness.
