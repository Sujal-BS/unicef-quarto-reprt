Vitamin A Supplementation: Saving Children's Lives Worldwide

Sujal Boodgere Sunil - A00096721 | BAA1030 - UNICEF Data Analyst Report

Executive Summary

Vitamin A deficiency is one of the leading preventable causes of childhood blindness and mortality in developing countries. Routine supplementation for children aged 6–59 months has been proven to reduce under-five mortality by up to 23%.

This report analyses global Vitamin A supplementation coverage (Semester 1) using UNICEF Indicator 1 and 2 datasets merged with World Bank metadata. It explores trends, economic correlations, and child mortality (ages 5–9) to raise awareness and support evidence-based action.

Key Findings & Insights

  • Global average coverage has shown long-term improvement since 2000, yet major regional disparities remain.
  • Higher GDP per capita is strongly positively correlated with better supplementation coverage (clear linear trend observed).
  • Several countries still have coverage rates below 10%, exposing millions of children to preventable risks.
  • A noticeable dip in coverage occurred in 2020 due to COVID-19 disruptions in health services.
  • Countries with persistently low coverage tend to show higher child mortality rates in the 5–9 age group.

Recommendations

To accelerate progress, UNICEF and national governments should prioritise:

  • Strengthening cold-chain logistics and supply reliability in low-coverage countries.
  • Integrating Vitamin A supplementation with routine immunisation and nutrition programmes.
  • Enhancing real-time monitoring and community engagement to improve delivery rates.
  • Targeted funding and technical support for countries with coverage below 50%.

Every additional percentage point in coverage can save thousands of young lives every year.
