Vitamin A Supplementation: Saving Children's Lives Worldwide

Sujal Boodgere Sunil - A00096721 | BAA1030 UNICEF Data Analyst Report

1. Executive Summary

Vitamin A deficiency is one of the leading preventable causes of childhood blindness and mortality. In my analysis, routine supplementation for children aged 6–59 months can reduce under-five mortality by up to 23%.

Using UNICEF data, I found that while global coverage has improved, significant gaps remain. This report examines these trends and their link to economic factors and child mortality.

2. Why This Issue Matters

From the data, I observed that millions of children in low-income countries still lack access to Vitamin A. This leads to preventable blindness and higher death rates from common infections. Supplementation is one of the most cost-effective ways to protect them.

3. Key Findings

  • In 2023, some countries achieved nearly 99% coverage (e.g., Bangladesh, Zambia), while others remained below 10% (e.g., Angola, Benin in certain years).
  • Higher GDP per capita is strongly associated with better supplementation rates — a clear positive trend in the scatterplot.
  • Coverage dropped noticeably in 2020 across many countries due to COVID-19 disruptions.
  • Low-coverage countries often show higher child mortality rates in the 5–9 age group.

4. Data & Methodology

I loaded and processed the data using the **Polars** library in Python. This included merging UNICEF Indicator 1 and Indicator 2 with World Bank metadata, filtering for total sex and relevant years, and calculating averages. All visualisations were created with the **plotnine** package.

6. Recommendations & Call to Action

Based on my analysis, I recommend:

  • Strengthening supply chains for reliable delivery in low-coverage countries.
  • Integrating supplementation with routine immunisation programmes.
  • Running community awareness campaigns to increase uptake.
  • Providing targeted funding to countries with coverage below 50%.

Every percentage point increase in coverage can save thousands of young lives.
