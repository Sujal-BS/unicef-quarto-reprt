1. Executive Summary

Vitamin A deficiency is a major yet preventable threat to child health worldwide. It causes blindness and weakens immunity, making children more vulnerable to infections. Simple twice-yearly Vitamin A supplements can reduce child mortality by up to 23% in children aged 6–59 months.

This report analyses global supplementation coverage using UNICEF data, explores its links with economic factors and child mortality, and provides clear recommendations to improve outcomes.

2. Why This Issue Matters

Every year, hundreds of thousands of children suffer or die from Vitamin A deficiency. Supplementation is one of the most cost-effective public health interventions available. By raising awareness and understanding current coverage gaps, we can support UNICEF and governments to reach every child who needs protection.

3. Key Findings

  • Global coverage has improved since 2000, but large disparities remain between regions.
  • Higher GDP per capita is strongly associated with better supplementation rates.
  • Several countries still have coverage below 10%, leaving millions of children at risk.
  • Coverage dropped noticeably in 2020 due to COVID-19 disruptions.
  • Low coverage often correlates with higher child mortality in the 5–9 age group.

4. Data & Methodology

Data from UNICEF Indicator 1 (Vitamin A coverage) and Indicator 2 (mortality 5–9) were merged with World Bank metadata using the Polars library in Python. Cleaning, filtering, grouping, and summarising were performed efficiently. All visualisations were created with the plotnine package for clear, reproducible results.

6. Recommendations & Call to Action

To save more lives, the following actions are recommended:

  • Strengthen supply chains for reliable delivery.
  • Integrate supplementation with routine immunisation.
  • Run community awareness campaigns to increase uptake.
  • Provide targeted funding and technical support to low-coverage countries.

Every percentage point increase in coverage can prevent thousands of child deaths annually.
